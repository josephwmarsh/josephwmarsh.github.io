## Project Overview
In the high-stakes environment of aerospace engineering, selecting a propulsion system is a complex decision involving competing priorities like cost, reliability, performance, and environmental impact. This project involved a rigorous **Trade Study** comparing **Solid Rocket Boosters (SRB)** and **Liquid Rocket Boosters (LRB)** for a heavy-lift launch vehicle.

### Analytical Methodology
To move beyond subjective preference, I applied a formal **Multi-Attribute Utility Theory (MAUT)** framework to evaluate the two systems:
* **Defining Attributes:** Established measurable criteria including Thrust-to-Weight ratio, Specific Impulse ($I_{sp}$), manufacturing complexity, and total lifecycle cost.
* **Weighting Factors:** Assigned importance values to each attribute based on mission-critical priorities (e.g., safety-critical reliability vs. mission-duration performance).
* **Sensitivity Analysis:** Conducted a sensitivity analysis to determine how changes in specific variables (like a 10% increase in propellant cost) would shift the optimal decision.

### Key Engineering Insights
* **Solid Boosters:** Found to be the optimal choice for missions requiring high initial thrust and lower upfront development costs, despite lower fuel efficiency.
* **Liquid Boosters:** Identified as the superior long-term investment for reusable launch architectures where high specific impulse and throttleability are required for precise landing and recovery.
* **Result:** Developed a quantitative decision matrix that justified a specific propulsion path based on a balanced "Cost-per-kg-to-Orbit" metric.
